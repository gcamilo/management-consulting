# Pricing Frameworks — Practitioner Reference

Five frameworks for setting and defending prices. Each solves a different pricing
problem: capturing differentiated value, ensuring cost coverage, positioning against
competitors, adapting to demand shifts, or building a user base at scale. Real-world
pricing almost always combines 2-3 of these. The question is not "which framework?"
but "which combination, and in what proportion?"

---

## 1. Value-Based Pricing

**Solves:** Leaving money on the table by pricing on cost or competition instead of
the economic value you create for the customer. Shifts buyer conversations from
"your price is too high" to "here's the ROI you'll capture."

**Does NOT solve:** Commodity markets where buyers see no differentiation, or new
categories where the value is speculative and unproven.

### When to Use

- You can quantify a measurable customer outcome (hours saved, revenue gained, risk reduced)
- Your product is differentiated — buyers cannot get the same result from 10 alternatives
- Deal sizes justify the research investment (B2B SaaS, consulting, enterprise services)
- You have (or can build) case studies with real numbers from deployments

**Skip it when:** The product is commoditized, value is speculative with no proof points,
or you sell high-volume self-serve with no sales conversation.

### Step-by-Step Workflow

1. **Segment by pain severity and willingness to pay.** Run 15-20 customer interviews
   plus a survey (100-200 responses). Identify 3-5 segments based on how acutely they
   feel the problem. A CFO cares about cost savings; a CTO cares about engineering hours.
   Output: named segment profiles with prioritized pain points.

2. **Quantify value drivers in dollar terms.** For each segment, identify 3-5 ways your
   product delivers measurable value. Work with product, sales, and customer success to
   translate into money.
   - "Reduces warehouse picking errors by 40%. Facility processing 10,000 orders/day
     at $12/error = $48,000/month saved."
   - "AI anomaly detection cuts false positives 70%, saving each analyst 15 hrs/week.
     At $60/hr fully loaded = $46,800/year per analyst."
   Output: Value Hypothesis Matrix — features mapped to dollar-denominated benefits per segment.

3. **Gather hard evidence.** Collect case studies, pilot data, and customer testimonials
   documenting real outcomes ("reduced inventory carrying costs by $200K/year"). Without
   documented proof, the price collapses when procurement pushes back. Minimum: 3 case
   studies with quantified outcomes before any price increase.

4. **Set a value-to-price ratio.** Capture 10-25% of the value you create. If you save
   a customer $500K/year, price at $50K-$125K. This gives the buyer a 4-10x ROI, making
   internal justification straightforward.
   - Example: $95K/year total value (labor savings + risk reduction). Competitive market
     with some alternatives. Target 20-30% capture = $19K-$28.5K/year price range.

5. **Build tiered pricing.** Offer 3 tiers (good/better/best) aligned to different value
   levels. The customer picks scope, not discount. Map features to tiers based on their
   contribution to value, not their development cost.
   - "Pro: $24,000/year (~25% of value) for up to 5 analysts, unlimited alerts, standard
     integrations. Enterprise: custom pricing for >5 analysts, advanced integrations, SLA."

6. **Arm sales with a value calculator.** Build a simple tool (spreadsheet or interactive)
   that plugs in customer-specific inputs and outputs projected ROI. This shifts every
   negotiation from price justification to outcome discussion.

7. **Test and iterate.** Track win rates, deal size, and discount frequency by segment.
   Win rate below 30% = price too high or value story unconvincing. Win rate above 70% =
   you are underpricing. Re-run value quantification annually, especially before renewals.

### Good vs. Bad Output

**Good:**
> "Our platform reduces warehouse picking errors by 40%, which for a facility processing
> 10,000 orders/day at $12/error saves $48,000/month. We price at $9,500/month — a 5x
> ROI payback within the first 30 days."

Why it works: specific outcome, tied to the customer's actual operations, quantified,
with a clear ROI ratio.

**Bad:**
> "Our platform is the best in the market and provides tremendous value to warehouse
> operations. Pricing starts at $9,500/month."

Why it fails: "value" does zero work without a number attached. No quantification, no
connection to customer economics, no evidence.

**Good (consulting engagement):**
> "This engagement redesigns your procurement workflow. Based on our audit, you're losing
> $1.2M/year to maverick spending. Our fee is $180K — if we achieve even half the projected
> savings, you see a 3.3x return in year one."

**Bad (consulting engagement):**
> "Our rate is $350/hour. We estimate 500 hours, totaling $175K."

Why it fails: hourly pricing dressed up as a proposal. The client sees cost, not outcome.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Stopping at qualitative benefits.** "Improves efficiency" is not value-based pricing.
   The real work is translating "better" into "$X saved" or "$Y gained." Requires deep
   customer empathy and access to their internal cost data.

2. **No sales enablement.** Building a value model but failing to equip sales with ROI
   calculators, value stories, and objection-handling scripts. Product teams understand
   the value; sales and customers need it made explicit.

3. **Ignoring the next best alternative.** Pricing solely on your value without considering
   what the buyer would do otherwise — competitor, in-house build, or "do nothing." The NBA
   sets the true baseline for perceived value.

4. **Letting procurement reframe the conversation.** Procurement's job is to deny
   differentiation and push commodity pricing. If the value story hasn't been sold to the
   economic buyer before procurement engages, the price erodes. Sell value to the business
   stakeholder first; give procurement a structured concession plan, not an open negotiation.

5. **Stale value models on renewals.** The value calculation from 2 years ago no longer
   reflects current capabilities or the customer's current pain. Re-quantify annually.

### When It Breaks Down

- **Commodity products with many substitutes.** If the customer sees 10 interchangeable
  vendors, perceived differentiation is zero and value-based degrades into competitive pricing.
- **New categories where value is speculative.** Customers have no reference point. You cannot
  quantify savings from a problem they don't know they have. Use pilot-based pricing first.
- **Complex B2B with 6+ stakeholders.** No single "value" to price against. The value story
  must be tailored per stakeholder — expensive and slow.
- **High-volume, low-touch self-serve.** Value-based pricing requires a conversation. At
  10,000 units/month through self-serve, use value research to set the price, but the sale
  is price-listed, not negotiated.

### Best Combinations

| Pair With | How | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Competitive** | Competitive sets floor/ceiling; value positions within the range | B2B SaaS where buyers comparison-shop but differentiation exists |
| **Freemium** | Free tier demonstrates value; paid tier captures it | PLG companies where usage creates measurable outcomes |
| **Cost-Plus** | Cost-plus sets the absolute floor; value-based sets the target | Hardware + software bundles, professional services |
| **Dynamic** | Base price from value; adjustments for demand, urgency, capacity | Consulting, enterprise SaaS with variable deal complexity |

## 2. Cost-Plus Pricing

**Solves:** Guaranteeing a positive margin on every unit sold. Provides transparency,
simplicity, and defensibility — particularly in regulated or government contexts.

**Does NOT solve:** Revenue optimization, competitive positioning, or capturing willingness
to pay. Cost-plus tells you nothing about demand.

### When to Use

- Margins must be auditable and transparent (government contracts, regulated industries)
- Products are commoditized with little differentiation
- Cost structure is well-understood and relatively stable
- You need a pricing floor for a hybrid model (cost-plus as floor + value-based as target)

**Skip it when:** Marginal cost is near zero (software, digital), the product is highly
differentiated, or competitors have structurally lower costs.

**Formula:** Selling Price = Total Unit Cost x (1 + Markup %)

### Step-by-Step Workflow

1. **Calculate direct costs per unit.** Materials, components, direct labor (separate
   assembly, QA, fulfillment), packaging, shipping. Be granular — "labor" is not one number.

2. **Allocate indirect costs (overhead).** Rent, utilities, insurance, management salaries,
   depreciation, IT, compliance. Allocation methods: per-unit (total overhead / units
   produced), percentage-of-direct-cost, or activity-based costing (ABC).
   Use ABC if overhead exceeds 40% of total cost.

3. **Calculate total unit cost.** Direct + allocated indirect per unit. Validate: total unit
   cost x expected volume should approximate total costs. Include the items most teams forget:
   returns/warranty (1-3%), payment processing (2-3%), customer acquisition cost amortization,
   regulatory compliance, and cost of capital tied up in inventory. A 5% cost undercount on a
   20% markup means you actually earn 15% margin — 25% less profit than planned.

4. **Set markup percentage.** Benchmark against industry norms:
   - Retail: 50-100%
   - Manufacturing: 10-35%
   - Food service: 200-300%
   - SaaS: 70-90%
   Adjust for competitive intensity, volume, brand strength, and channel (wholesale vs. direct).

5. **Validate against market.** Is the resulting price within the range customers expect?
   If cost-plus output is wildly out of market range, you have a cost problem or a markup
   problem — not a pricing strategy.

6. **Build a review cadence.** Review input costs monthly for volatile categories (commodities,
   logistics), quarterly for stable ones. Price on replacement cost (what it costs to
   replenish today), not historical cost. Automate cost tracking — manual spreadsheets mean
   stale data.

### Good vs. Bad Output

**Good:**
> "Unit COGS: $42.30 (materials $28.10, labor $9.20, packaging $5.00). Overhead allocation:
> $11.70/unit. Total unit cost: $54.00. At 35% markup, retail price = $72.90. Market comp
> range: $65-$85, so mid-market positioning. Review trigger: if materials cost moves +/-10%,
> reprice within 2 weeks."

Why it works: transparent breakdown, justified markup, market-validated, with a review trigger.

**Bad:**
> "Our costs are about $50 per unit. We add 40%. Price is $70."

Why it fails: "about $50" signals imprecise costing. No breakdown, no overhead allocation,
no market validation, no review plan. A guess with a markup.

**Good (services/contracting):**
> "Materials + subcontractor costs passed through at actual. Our markup: 15% on materials,
> 20% on labor. Monthly cost reconciliation with client. Cap: total project cost NTE $285K
> without change order."

**Bad (services):**
> "We charge cost plus 20% on everything."

Why it fails: no differentiation between cost categories, no cap, no reconciliation — invites
disputes and offers no incentive to control costs.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Incomplete cost capture.** The single most common failure. Teams forget returns/warranty,
   payment processing fees (2-3%), CAC amortization, compliance costs, and cost of capital
   in inventory. A 5% undercount on a 20% markup = 25% less profit than planned.

2. **Historical costs instead of replacement costs.** If you price on last quarter's
   materials and commodity prices rose 8%, your margin erodes in real time. Price on what
   it costs to replenish today.

3. **Uniform markup across all products.** 30% on a commodity item may be uncompetitive;
   30% on a differentiated accessory leaves money on the table. Use category-level or
   SKU-level markups informed by competitive positioning.

4. **No cost reduction incentive.** Cost-plus guarantees margin regardless of efficiency.
   Boeing and Lockheed Martin have delivered billions in cost overruns on cost-plus defense
   contracts. Pair cost-plus with explicit cost reduction targets.

5. **Ignoring price elasticity.** If customers would pay $120 but cost-plus yields $73,
   you give away $47 in potential margin. If cost-plus yields $73 but customers see $55
   value, you will not sell. Cost-plus is blind to demand.

### When It Breaks Down

- **High-margin, differentiated products.** Software, luxury goods, consulting — where
  production cost has little relationship to value delivered. Pricing SaaS at cost-plus
  yields absurd prices because marginal cost is near zero.
- **Rapidly changing input costs.** If commodities swing 20% month-over-month, your
  cost-plus price always lags. By the time you recalculate, you've sold at the wrong price.
- **Network-effect businesses.** Sometimes the correct price is below cost to build a user
  base. Cost-plus cannot express that strategic logic.
- **Competitors with structurally lower costs.** If a competitor manufactures at 60% of
  your cost (offshore production), cost-plus on YOUR costs produces a price the market
  will not bear. The framework doesn't tell you this — it just outputs a number.
- **Multi-product businesses with shared overhead.** Allocating shared costs across products
  is inherently arbitrary. Different allocation methods produce different unit costs and
  different prices. "True cost" is a fiction in complex organizations.

### Best Combinations

| Pair With | How | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Value-Based (as target)** | Cost-plus ensures you never sell below cost; value captures upside | The most common and effective hybrid. B2B, services, complex products |
| **Dynamic (as ceiling)** | Cost determines the minimum; demand determines how far above | Retail, e-commerce, perishable goods |
| **Competitive** | Calculate cost-plus price, then adjust if it falls outside competitive range | Manufacturing, wholesale, commodity-adjacent products |
| **Freemium** | Cost-plus analysis sizes the free tier (what can you afford to give away?) | SaaS with meaningful per-user infrastructure costs |

## 3. Competitive Pricing

**Solves:** Neutralizing price as a purchase objection by positioning relative to known
alternatives. Prevents being priced out of consideration sets.

**Does NOT solve:** Margin optimization, differentiation, or strategic pricing for unique
products. Following competitors assumes they did their homework — they may not have.

### When to Use

- Buyers actively comparison-shop and pricing is relatively transparent
- Market has 5+ direct competitors with comparable offerings
- Your product has moderate (not zero, not dominant) differentiation
- You need to respond to a competitive price move and lack time for full value analysis

**Skip it when:** You hold a monopoly/near-monopoly, your product is genuinely unique,
or competitors are VC-subsidized and pricing irrationally.

### Step-by-Step Workflow

1. **Define the competitive set from the buyer's perspective.** Identify 5-8 direct
   competitors (same product category, same buyer) and 3-5 indirect (different product,
   same job-to-be-done). Ask sales: "Who do we lose deals to?" Ask customers: "Who else
   did you evaluate?" Do not track 30+ competitors — you cannot act on that much data.

2. **Collect pricing data.** Sources: competitor websites, pricing intelligence tools
   (Prisync, Competera, Price2Spy), mystery shopping, win/loss interviews, channel partner
   intel. Capture not just price points but price structure: per-seat vs. per-usage,
   annual vs. monthly, bundled vs. unbundled.

3. **Normalize for apples-to-apples comparison.** Adjust for features included, contract
   terms, support level, implementation costs, and total cost of ownership (TCO).
   - Competitor A: $50/seat/month but includes onboarding worth $5K.
   - Competitor B: $40/seat/month but charges $10K for onboarding.
   - At 50 seats over 12 months: A = $30K total. B = $34K total. B is actually more
     expensive despite the lower sticker price.

4. **Choose positioning strategy.**
   - **Below market (penetration):** Viable only with a structural cost advantage or
     willingness to sacrifice margin for share. Dangerous without a cost moat.
   - **At market (parity):** Neutralize price as a factor, compete on features/service/brand.
     Safest default for most companies.
   - **Above market (premium):** Requires demonstrable differentiation paired with a value
     story. Without justification, premium pricing just loses deals.

5. **Set response rules and guardrails.** Define how you respond to competitor price changes.
   Set a floor below which you will not follow a competitor down (typically cost-plus minimum
   margin). Escalation rule: "If competitor drops >15%, escalate to pricing committee within
   48 hours."

6. **Monitor continuously.** Weekly for e-commerce, monthly for B2B. Track: competitor price
   changes, new entrants, feature changes, and your own win/loss rates by competitor. Review
   the competitive set quarterly — the set from 12 months ago may be stale.

### Good vs. Bad Output

**Good:**
> "Competitive analysis (Q1 2026): 6 direct competitors analyzed. Price range for comparable
> tier: $35-$62/seat/month. Median: $47. Our price: $52 — positioned 11% above median,
> justified by unique Salesforce integration (saves ~4 hrs/week per rep). Win rate vs. this
> set: 38%, stable QoQ. Price is not the primary loss reason — implementation timeline is.
> Recommendation: hold at $52."

Why it works: specific data, normalized comparison, positioning rationale tied to
differentiation, connected to win/loss data, actionable.

**Bad:**
> "We looked at competitor prices. Most charge $35-$60. We should price around $45 to be
> competitive."

Why it fails: no normalization, no rationale, no connection to differentiation or win/loss.
"Around $45" is defaulting to the middle, not a strategy.

**Good (retail):**
> "Wireless earbuds $50-$100 segment. Tracked 8 SKUs across 4 competitors weekly. Our unit
> at $79 is 5% below Apple ($84) and 12% above Samsung ($71). Elasticity testing: -0.3%
> volume per 1% price increase. Dropping to $71 gains ~2.4% volume but sacrifices $8/unit
> margin — net negative. Recommendation: hold."

**Bad (retail):**
> "Samsung dropped to $71. We should match."

Why it fails: reactive, no elasticity analysis, no margin impact. Matching without
understanding why they cut (clearance? new model? loss leader?) starts a race to the bottom.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Tracking the wrong competitors.** Monitoring 20 competitors including irrelevant ones
   dilutes focus. Worse: price-matching an enterprise competitor when you sell to SMBs
   misprices for your actual buyers. Define competitors from the customer's perspective.

2. **Assuming competitor prices are rational.** Their price may reflect a mistake, promotional
   clearance, VC-subsidized growth, or a different cost structure. Matching assumes they did
   their homework. Understand the "why" behind their price, not just the number.

3. **Triggering a race to the bottom.** You cut. They cut. Margins compress. The market
   learns to wait for discounts. Especially destructive in oligopolies. Compete on service,
   speed, or bundling. If you must respond, use a temporary promotion, not a permanent cut.

4. **Neglecting your own cost structure.** If your costs are $40/unit and you match at $42,
   you earn 5% margin while the competitor (at $30 cost) earns 29%. You cannot sustain that.
   Always validate competitive prices against your cost floor.

### When It Breaks Down

- **Monopoly or near-monopoly.** Competitive pricing gives you nothing useful. Use
  value-based pricing.
- **Rapidly innovating markets.** If the category changes every 6 months, last quarter's
  benchmarks are stale before you act on them.
- **VC-subsidized competitors.** Following a company pricing below cost to grab share
  destroys your business while they burn investor money. You cannot out-subsidize a company
  that doesn't need profitability.
- **Differentiated or niche products.** If you solve a problem no competitor addresses,
  competitive pricing anchors to an irrelevant comparison set.
- **High-variability services.** A $200/hr consultant who solves the problem in 10 hours
  is cheaper than a $100/hr consultant who takes 30. Price comparison is misleading.

### Best Combinations

| Pair With | How | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Value-Based** | Competitive sets the range; value positions within it | Most B2B/B2C with moderate differentiation |
| **Cost-Plus Floor** | Never go below cost floor, even if a competitor does | Commodity and retail markets |
| **Dynamic** | Competitor prices feed as one signal into a dynamic algorithm | E-commerce, travel, hospitality |
| **Freemium** | Match/undercut competitors' free tier; differentiate on paid | Crowded SaaS (project management, CRM) |

## 4. Dynamic Pricing

**Solves:** Revenue left on the table by static prices when demand, supply, competition,
and time all fluctuate. Captures willingness to pay in real-time rather than at the average.

**Does NOT solve:** Relationship-based B2B sales, products with stable demand, or categories
where consumers have strong fairness expectations (food, medicine, basic goods).

### When to Use

- High transaction volume (>1,000 transactions/day per SKU to learn patterns)
- Demand varies meaningfully by time, season, event, or capacity utilization
- Pricing is digital and can be updated programmatically
- You have (or can build) a real-time data pipeline

**Skip it when:** You sell to 50 enterprise accounts with negotiated contracts, demand
barely fluctuates, or regulatory constraints cap your pricing flexibility.

### Step-by-Step Workflow

1. **Define the optimization objective and hard constraints.** Revenue maximization, margin
   maximization, inventory clearance, or market share — these conflict, so pick one primary
   objective. Define constraints: price gouging laws, brand positioning (luxury brands cannot
   flash-sale), contractual obligations (price-match guarantees, MAP policies).

2. **Build the data pipeline.** Required: historical transaction data (minimum 6-12 months),
   real-time demand signals (web traffic, cart additions, search volume), competitor prices,
   inventory levels, time/calendar data, and customer segment data. This step is what most
   companies underestimate. A weekly dashboard is not dynamic pricing — it is slow repricing
   with stale data.

3. **Set guardrails before anything else.**
   - **Price floor:** Total unit cost + minimum margin. Non-negotiable.
   - **Price ceiling:** Cap automated increases. Common rule: no increase >20% above
     30-day rolling average.
   - **Rate-of-change limit:** Max 5% change per day to prevent jarring swings.
   - **Channel consistency:** Define acceptable variance between website, marketplace,
     and in-store.
   - **Sensitivity rules:** Automatically hold flat during emergencies, PR-sensitive periods,
     or when affecting vulnerable populations. (Uber's surge pricing during snowstorms and
     terrorist attacks generated lasting brand damage.)

4. **Choose the algorithm approach (start simple).**
   - **Rule-based (start here):** "If inventory < 10%, increase 10%. If competitor drops
     >5%, match within 3%. If demand index > 1.5x baseline, increase 15%." Transparent,
     auditable, easy to override.
   - **Regression models:** Price elasticity estimation from historical data. Good for
     products with stable demand patterns.
   - **Reinforcement learning:** Algorithm experiments with prices and learns. Most powerful,
     least transparent. Requires >10K transactions/day per SKU.
   Recommendation: start rule-based. Graduate to regression after 6+ months of clean data.

5. **Pilot before full rollout.** One product category or one region. A/B test: dynamic vs.
   static on comparable segments. Measure: revenue per unit, total margin, customer
   satisfaction (NPS/CSAT), and return rate. Minimum pilot: 30-60 days to capture buying
   cycle variation.

6. **Communicate and monitor.**
   - Customer-facing: "Prices may vary based on demand and availability. During off-peak
     times, you may find lower prices." Transparency builds trust.
   - Internal: train sales and support on how to explain price variation.
   - Monitor for: algorithmic errors outside guardrails, unintended discrimination by
     segment, and competitive retaliation patterns.
   - Quarterly audits on pricing outcomes by customer segment.

### Good vs. Bad Output

**Good (pilot report):**
> "Dynamic pricing pilot (90 days, outdoor furniture): Revenue +11.2%, margin +8.4%, ASP
> +6.7%. Floor held at COGS+18%. Ceiling at 20% above 30-day avg. 3 override events:
> July 4th surge, Hurricane Milton (held flat per brand guidelines), competitor fire sale
> (held floor). Customer pricing complaints: 2 (vs. 47 about shipping). Recommend expanding
> to 3 additional categories."

Why it works: data-driven, guardrails defined and tested, edge cases handled deliberately,
customer impact measured.

**Bad:**
> "We implemented dynamic pricing. Revenue is up 15%!"

Why it fails: no margin data (revenue up but margin down?), no guardrail discussion, no
customer impact measurement, no pilot methodology. Could be masking a pricing disaster.

**The Wendy's cautionary tale:**
> CEO on earnings call: "We're implementing dynamic pricing with digital menu boards."
> Headlines: "Wendy's to introduce Uber-style surge pricing on burgers."
> Result: #BoycottWendys trends nationally. Burger King launches "No urge to surge."

Lesson: how you frame dynamic pricing matters as much as the algorithm. "Surge pricing"
on everyday food triggers outrage. "Happy hour discounts" on the same mechanism is welcomed.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Deploying without guardrails.** An unconstrained algorithm will find the revenue-maximizing
   price, which may be a 300% markup during a supply shortage. Hard-code floors, ceilings, and
   rate-of-change limits before going live. No exceptions.

2. **Optimizing the wrong metric.** Revenue without margin constraints = volume at negative
   margin. Margin alone = collapsed volume. Define a composite objective (e.g., maximize margin
   subject to volume floor) and monitor both.

3. **Insufficient data infrastructure.** If your pipeline has a 24-hour lag, you are doing
   daily repricing with stale data, not dynamic pricing. Invest in the pipeline before the
   algorithm. The algorithm is the easy part.

4. **Full-scale launch without pilot.** Across all SKUs simultaneously means you cannot
   isolate what works. If something breaks, you cannot find the cause. Always pilot with
   limited scope for 30-60 days.

5. **Ignoring customer perception.** Technically optimal prices that destroy trust are not
   optimal. Build sensitivity rules into the algorithm — cap prices or hold flat during
   emergencies and PR-sensitive periods.

### When It Breaks Down

- **Stable, predictable demand.** A 2% revenue lift may not justify the infrastructure cost.
- **Relationship-based B2B.** Visible price changes undermine trust with enterprise accounts.
  The algorithm may be right that Account X would pay 15% more, but the relationship damage
  from testing it exceeds the gain.
- **Everyday essentials.** Consumers have strong fairness expectations about food, medicine,
  and basic goods. Backlash is disproportionate to revenue gain.
- **Low-volume, high-value products.** At 50 units/month, the algorithm has insufficient data
  to converge. Use value-based or competitive pricing.
- **Regulated industries.** Healthcare, utilities, insurance — legal constraints may limit
  pricing flexibility to the point where infrastructure cost exceeds the flexibility gained.

### Best Combinations

| Pair With | How | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost-Plus Floor** | Cost sets the floor; demand determines how far above | Standard config for e-commerce and retail |
| **Competitive Intelligence** | Competitor prices as one signal in the algorithm | Markets with observable, influential competitor pricing |
| **Value-Based Ceiling** | Value quantification sets the maximum; dynamic operates within | B2B SaaS with usage-based pricing |
| **Freemium** | Free tier is static; paid tier uses dynamic pricing by usage/capacity | Cloud computing, marketplace platforms |

## 5. Freemium Model

**Solves:** Customer acquisition at scale by removing the purchase barrier. The free tier
is both acquisition channel and live product demo. Revenue comes from converting a
fraction of free users to paid.

**Does NOT solve:** Monetization of small addressable markets (<5,000 potential customers),
high-marginal-cost products, or complex products requiring human onboarding.

### When to Use

- Large addressable market (>100K potential users) to make 2-5% conversion viable
- Product delivers clear value in self-serve without human assistance
- Marginal cost per free user is low (<$1/month)
- Natural usage growth creates upgrade triggers (need more seats, storage, features)

**Skip it when:** TAM is small (5,000 companies), each user costs $5-10/month to serve,
or the product requires a 2-hour onboarding call to deliver value.

### Step-by-Step Workflow

1. **Define the value metric.** The unit of consumption that scales with customer value:
   seats, storage, API calls, projects, messages, contacts. The right metric grows naturally
   as value grows. Wrong: charging per login (doesn't scale with value). Right: charging per
   active user (scales with team adoption).

2. **Set the free tier boundary.** The 80/20 rule: provide ~80% of core functionality but
   only ~20% of the value metric capacity. Target: 20-40% of users fully satisfied with free.
   If >50% never need to upgrade, the free tier is too generous. If <10% find it useful,
   it is too restrictive for adoption.
   Three gating strategies:
   - **Feature gating:** Core features free, advanced paid (Slack: limited message history).
   - **Usage gating:** All features, but capped (Dropbox: 2GB free).
   - **Outcome gating:** Free for individual, paid for collaboration (Figma: free solo, paid teams).

3. **Design upgrade triggers.** Identify 2-3 moments when a free user hits a genuine
   limitation through natural usage. "I need more storage." "I need to add a team member."
   "I need the advanced report." These should feel like a natural next step, not a punishment.
   Too many triggers and the free experience feels crippled; too few and users never hit the wall.

4. **Build the conversion path.** In-app prompts at the moment of the trigger (not random
   pop-ups). Show what they are missing: "Your team has 3 pending invites. Upgrade to add
   them." Reduce friction: one-click upgrade, pre-filled billing, prorated charges,
   immediate access. Offer a limited-time preview of premium features before requiring payment.

5. **Instrument and measure.**
   - Free-to-paid conversion: 2-5% typical, 5-10% excellent
   - Time-to-convert: days from signup to first payment
   - Activation rate: % completing a key action within first 7 days
   - Free user cost: hosting, support, email per user per month
   - LTV:CAC for converted users: should be >3:1
   Track at 30, 60, and 90-day intervals.

6. **Iterate the boundary.** Conversion <1%: free tier too generous, tighten. Conversion >10%
   but growth slow: too restrictive for adoption. A/B test different limits, prompts, and
   paywall placements. Expect to adjust quarterly.

### Good vs. Bad Output

**Good (tier design):**
> "Free: 3 projects, 1 user, 100MB storage, community support. Pro ($12/mo): unlimited
> projects, 5 users, 10GB, email support. Team ($29/user/mo): Pro + SSO, audit logs, API,
> priority support. Upgrade trigger: user creates 4th project (hits 70% of free users by
> day 30). Conversion target: 4% at 90 days."

Why it works: clear feature differentiation, useful but bounded free tier, trigger tied to
natural usage, measurable target.

**Bad (tier design):**
> "Free: basic features. Pro ($15/mo): more features. Enterprise: contact us."

Why it fails: no specificity, no value metric, no trigger, no target.

**Good (conversion analysis):**
> "Free base: 45,000. Paid conversions (90 days): 1,890 (4.2%). Median time-to-convert:
> 23 days. Top trigger: team invite limit (62% of conversions). Cost per free user:
> $0.34/mo. Converted user LTV: $840. Blended CAC: $112. LTV:CAC = 7.5:1. Recommendation:
> tighten storage from 500MB to 200MB (second-highest infra cost but only 8% of conversions)."

**Bad (conversion analysis):**
> "We have 45,000 free users and 1,890 paying customers. We need to convert more."

Why it fails: no conversion rate context, no trigger analysis, no cost analysis.
"Convert more" is a wish, not a strategy.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Free tier too generous.** The NYT offered 20 free articles/month — most readers never
   needed more. Cut to 10, then 5, conversions rose each time. Evernote allowed 100,000
   free notes — virtually unlimited. Start more restrictive than you think. Loosening limits
   generates goodwill; tightening generates outrage.

2. **No clear upgrade trigger.** If free users can accomplish everything without hitting a
   wall, they never convert. Map the user journey. Identify 2-3 moments where expanded
   capability delivers meaningful additional value. Gate those moments.

3. **Underestimating free user costs.** At 100K+ free users at $0.30/mo, you spend $360K/year
   serving users who may never pay. Model free user cost explicitly. If cost exceeds what
   conversion revenue supports at realistic rates, freemium may not be viable.

4. **Treating free users as second-class.** If the free experience is buggy, slow, or
   deliberately degraded, users churn before converting. The free tier IS your marketing.
   The free product should be excellent — just limited in scope.

5. **Neglecting activation over acquisition.** Users who engage with core features in the
   first week are 5x more likely to convert. Most products invest in signups and neglect
   onboarding. First-week experience — setup wizards, "aha moment" acceleration — is a
   higher-ROI investment than ad spend.

### When It Breaks Down

- **High marginal cost per user ($5-10/month).** The math of 2-5% conversion rarely works.
  Need extremely high LTV or conversion rate to justify serving free users.
- **Small addressable market.** If TAM is 5,000 companies, giving away the product to 4,750
  and monetizing 250 is not a business model. Use sales-led value-based pricing.
- **Complex, high-touch products.** If value requires onboarding, training, or customization,
  freemium creates a support burden without conversion.
- **Free tier cannibalizes paid.** If free users include people who would have paid anyway,
  freemium does not create incremental customers — it discounts people who did not need it.
  Common when existing paid customers downgrade.
- **Commoditized market with many free alternatives.** If 5 competitors offer comparable
  free tiers, users have no reason to pay any of you. Freemium becomes a race to give
  away the most.

### Best Combinations

| Pair With | How | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Value-Based (paid tiers)** | Free for acquisition; paid priced on quantified value per segment | PLG SaaS with measurable customer outcomes |
| **Usage-Based / Dynamic** | Free up to threshold; paid scales with usage | API products, cloud infra, developer tools |
| **Competitive** | Free tier matches competitors' free; paid differentiates | Crowded SaaS categories |
| **Cost-Plus (free tier sizing)** | Cost analysis determines what you can afford to give away at target rates | Products with meaningful per-user infrastructure costs |

## Quick Reference: When to Use Which

| Criterion | Value-Based | Cost-Plus | Competitive | Dynamic | Freemium |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Best for** | Differentiated B2B, services | Commodities, government | Transparent markets, retail | High-volume, variable demand | Digital products, SaaS |
| **Data required** | Customer research, outcomes | Cost accounting | Competitor intelligence | Transaction history, real-time demand | Usage analytics, conversion |
| **Time to implement** | 3-6 months | 2-4 weeks | 4-8 weeks | 3-6 months (with pilot) | 2-4 months |
| **Complexity** | High | Low | Medium | High | Medium |
| **Underprice risk** | Low | High | Medium | Low | High |
| **Overprice risk** | Medium | Medium | Low | Medium | Low |

## Five Universal Principles

1. **Know your costs regardless of framework.** Even value-based pricing needs a cost floor.
2. **Pricing is never a one-time decision.** Static prices in a dynamic market = slow-motion error.
3. **Segment before you price.** One price for all customers is almost always wrong.
4. **Measure margin, not just revenue.** A price that maximizes revenue while destroying margin or trust is not a good price.
5. **Hybrid is the default.** Cost-plus for the floor, competitive for the range, value-based for positioning within it. The question is always "which combination?"
