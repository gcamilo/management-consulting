# Variant: Commercial Due Diligence

**Mode:** Full Case (or Client Deliverable if an IC memo / red-flags pack is requested).

## When to use

The user is evaluating an acquisition target, investment opportunity, or partnership -- and needs to assess the commercial viability of the business.

## Context to establish (assume if missing, flag as `[A]`)

If the user hasn't specified, state assumptions upfront and proceed. Do NOT stall waiting for answers.

1. **What's the target?** Company name, sector, stage, approximate size.
2. **What's the deal thesis?** Why is this interesting? (Growth, synergy, distressed asset, platform play.)
3. **What's the timeline?** When does the decision need to be made?
4. **What data do we have?** CIM, financials, customer list, management access?
5. **What would kill the deal?** Pre-commit to 2-3 dealbreakers before starting -- if the user hasn't named them, propose plausible ones and let them push back.

## Commercial DD workflow

### 1. Customer analysis

- **Concentration risk:** Top 10 customers as % of revenue. Flag if top customer >15% or top 3 >40%.
- **Cohort retention:** Revenue retention by customer cohort (year of acquisition). Improving or decaying?
- **NPS / satisfaction signal:** Any available data on customer sentiment. If none, that's a red flag to note.
- **Reference checks:** What would you ask the target's top 5 customers?

### 2. Revenue quality

- **Recurring vs one-time:** What % of revenue is contractually recurring (ARR) vs transactional?
- **Revenue durability:** Average contract length, renewal rates, pricing power (ability to raise prices).
- **Pipeline health:** Weighted pipeline as multiple of quota. Sales cycle length trend.
- **Accounting flags:** Revenue recognition policy, deferred revenue trends, unbilled AR.

### 3. Competitive moat

- **Switching costs:** How hard is it for customers to leave? (Data lock-in, integration depth, workflow dependency.)
- **Network effects:** Does the product get better with more users?
- **IP / proprietary advantage:** Patents, proprietary data, unique algorithms.
- **Brand and trust:** Especially in regulated industries or enterprise sales.

### 4. Management assessment

- **Founder/CEO quality:** Track record, domain expertise, ambition vs capability.
- **Team depth:** Key-person risk. What happens if the top 3 people leave?
- **Culture signals:** Glassdoor, turnover rate, hiring velocity.

### 5. Market position

- **Market growth:** Is the target riding a growing wave or fighting for share in a flat market?
- **Competitive dynamics:** Who's gaining share? Who's losing? Why?
- **Regulatory tailwinds/headwinds:** Any upcoming regulation that helps or hurts?

## Required deliverables

1. **Risk matrix** -- 2x2 (likelihood x impact) with top 8-10 risks plotted and color-coded.
2. **Revenue quality scorecard** -- summary table grading each dimension (A/B/C/F).
3. **Customer concentration chart** -- bar chart showing revenue by customer, Pareto-style.
4. **Investment recommendation** -- proceed / proceed with conditions / walk away.

## Red flags checklist

Watch for these. Any single one warrants deeper investigation; 3+ together suggest "walk away."

- [ ] Top customer >25% of revenue
- [ ] NRR below 90% (SaaS) or declining YoY retention
- [ ] Revenue growth driven by one-time or non-recurring sources
- [ ] Key-person dependency (1-2 people hold all critical relationships)
- [ ] No clear competitive moat -- product is "good enough" but substitutable
- [ ] Management unable to articulate why customers stay
- [ ] Material customer complaints or litigation in progress
- [ ] Unusual revenue recognition (front-loaded, channel-stuffed, or heavy deferred)
- [ ] High employee turnover in customer-facing roles (>30% annual)
- [ ] Market is shrinking or consolidating around larger players

## Common mistakes in DD

- Falling in love with the thesis and seeking confirming evidence.
- Trusting management's narrative without independent customer validation.
- Analyzing the target in isolation instead of relative to alternatives (other acquisitions, organic build, do nothing).
- Underweighting integration risk -- the deal might be great but the integration might destroy value.
